## Why I Made the Switch

After nearly three years at McKinsey and two at EY-Parthenon before that, I had built a strong foundation in commercial due diligence, market analysis, and strategic problem-solving. But I found myself increasingly drawn to the other side of the table—making investment decisions rather than just informing them.

## Skills That Transfer Seamlessly

### Analytical Rigor

The structured problem-solving approach that consulting drills into you is invaluable in investing. Building market models, sizing TAMs, evaluating competitive dynamics—these are the same muscles, just applied in a different context.

### Stakeholder Management

Working with C-suite executives and managing client relationships in consulting prepares you well for working with portfolio company management teams and co-investors.

### Pattern Recognition

After dozens of due diligence engagements across sectors, you start to develop an intuition for what makes a great business. This pattern recognition is one of the most valuable things a consultant brings to an investing role.

## What Is Different

### Time Horizons

In consulting, you are optimizing for the next 8-12 weeks of a project. In investing, you are thinking in terms of 5-7 year hold periods. This shift in time horizon changes how you evaluate opportunities and make decisions.

### Skin in the Game

The biggest difference is ownership. When you are deploying capital into a business, the stakes are fundamentally different than when you are advising on a decision. This accountability sharpens your thinking in ways that consulting cannot replicate.

## Advice for Those Considering the Transition

If you are thinking about moving from consulting to investing, my advice is to lean into the transition rather than treating it as a lateral move. The skills transfer, but the mindset shift is real. Embrace it.
